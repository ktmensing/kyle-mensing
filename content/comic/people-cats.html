Connecting People and Cats

<%= publish_time_tag %>

I just finished reading this talk and can't recommend it highly enough.[1]

It touches on a lot of interesting points, mirrors a lot of my opinions,[2] and clearly lays out the different priorities that are trying to claim the web.[3]

While it's chock full of takeaways, these are the things that jumped out at me. Either directly from or inspired by parts of the piece:

  • Remember you are always operating within a system, you can do the coolest thing imaginable but if it doesn’t fit within the system it won’t go anywhere
  • We don’t owe corporations a continued existence for any reason if they are not serving us in some useful, meaningful way.[4]
  • What are you building this for? Who are you building this for?
  • If you do your work really well—and don’t let completely plausible excuses give you an out—you can build an industry’s future, even if you’re the B team
  • How many things we see as uniquely American are actually a bill of goods sold to us by someone seeking profit?[5]
  • “Good enough” is perfectly fine.[6]
  • Living in the future is no way to experience and fix the present
  • Growth is not an end game or state. And infinite growth is impossible. What happens when growth stops.[7]
  • What does this quote mean to you? “The future is here, it’s just not evenly distributed yet.”
  • Listen to the people who know what they’re talking about, try to find the motivations behind the people who don’t.[8]

I’m on team Connect Knowledge, People, and Cats.

So go find a hobby, something you’re curious about, something you want to learn, or simply a way to share whatever it is with others. Document how you do it. Use cheap or open source tools where possible[9] and share those resources with others.[10] Turn yourself and your knowledge into a platform for others to benefit from. And hey, maybe pet a cat (or other furry animal) today.

Source: Web Design: The First 100 Years
